CRM Systems

~15 min read  ·  8-question quiz

What Is a CRM?

A CRM (Customer Relationship Management) system is software that helps a company manage its relationships with current and potential customers. At its core, a CRM is a database — but one specifically designed around people, companies, and the interactions between them.

Before CRMs, salespeople tracked deals in spreadsheets, notebooks, or their own heads. That worked for small teams but fell apart at scale. A CRM gives the entire sales organization a shared, organized record of every contact, account, deal, and conversation.

Why this matters for you CRM is one of the most common categories of enterprise software. If you're consulting for a company that sells to other businesses, their sales team is almost certainly using a CRM — and your client may be evaluating, implementing, or customizing one.

Core CRM Objects

CRMs organize data around a few key "objects" — the building blocks of the system:

Contacts

Individual people. A contact record stores a person's name, title, email, phone, and their history with your company — every email sent, call logged, and meeting held.

Accounts

Companies (also called "organizations" in some CRMs). An account groups all the contacts who work at the same company, so you can see the full relationship with that business in one place.

Opportunities (Deals)

A specific sales deal. An opportunity tracks what product is being sold, the deal value, the expected close date, and which stage of the sales process it's in. Opportunities are what salespeople manage day-to-day.

Activities

Logged interactions: calls, emails, meetings, notes. Activities give a timeline of everything that's happened with a contact or deal. Good CRM hygiene means logging every touchpoint so anyone on the team can pick up a deal with full context.

The Sales Pipeline

One of the most important views in any CRM is the pipeline — a visual representation of all active deals organized by stage. Common stages include:

  1. Prospecting — identified a potential customer, no contact yet
  2. Qualified — confirmed the prospect has a need, budget, and timeline
  3. Demo / Discovery — actively presenting the product
  4. Proposal — formal pricing sent
  5. Negotiation — working out contract terms
  6. Closed Won / Closed Lost — deal is decided

Managers use pipeline views to forecast revenue, spot stalled deals, and coach their teams.

Major CRM Vendors

Salesforce

The dominant CRM in the enterprise market. Salesforce is a massive platform — not just a CRM but an entire ecosystem of tools for sales, marketing, service, and more. It's highly customizable, which is why there's a large industry of Salesforce consultants and developers. It's expensive and complex, which is also why many companies hire consultants to implement and manage it.

HubSpot

Originally a marketing tool, HubSpot expanded into a full CRM platform. It's more user-friendly than Salesforce and popular with mid-market companies. It offers a free CRM tier, which makes it easy to adopt, and paid tiers for more advanced features.

Microsoft Dynamics 365

Microsoft's CRM and ERP platform. It's tightly integrated with other Microsoft products (Outlook, Teams, Azure), which makes it attractive to enterprises already running on Microsoft infrastructure.

Pipedrive, Zoho, Freshsales

Smaller, more affordable CRMs popular with SMBs. They offer the core pipeline and contact management features without the complexity (or cost) of Salesforce.

How CRMs Connect to Other Systems

A CRM rarely works alone. In a modern enterprise, it connects to:

  • Marketing automation — so leads flow automatically into the CRM when someone fills out a form or attends a webinar
  • Email and calendar — so emails and meetings sync automatically without manual logging
  • ERP / billing — so when a deal closes, the contract and invoice flow to finance
  • Customer success platforms — so the CS team sees the full account history after the sale
  • Analytics / BI — so leadership can report on pipeline, win rates, and revenue forecasts
The CRM is the system of record for revenue Just as HR has a system of record for employee data, and finance has one for transactions, the CRM is the authoritative source of truth for everything related to customers and revenue. That's why CRM data quality is such a big deal — garbage in, garbage out.

CRM Challenges in the Enterprise

CRM implementations frequently fail or underperform. Common reasons:

  • Low adoption: Salespeople see the CRM as a reporting tool for management, not a tool that helps them. If entering data feels like extra work with no personal benefit, they won't do it.
  • Bad data: Duplicate records, outdated contacts, and incomplete fields make the CRM unreliable. Companies often need a dedicated "RevOps" team to manage data quality.
  • Over-customization: Enterprises often customize Salesforce so heavily it becomes hard to upgrade or maintain.
  • Integration failures: If the CRM doesn't sync cleanly with marketing, billing, or support tools, data lives in silos and the CRM loses value.
