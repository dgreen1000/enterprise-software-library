Module 4

Marketing & the MarTech Stack

~15 min read  ·  8-question quiz

What Enterprise Marketing Teams Do

Marketing's job is to create awareness, generate interest, and produce qualified leads that the sales team can close. In a B2B enterprise context, this is a complex, multi-channel operation involving content, events, advertising, email campaigns, and more.

Unlike B2C marketing (where you're trying to convince an individual to buy), B2B marketing targets entire companies and needs to reach multiple stakeholders — each with different concerns. The CMO cares about ROI. The IT buyer cares about security. The end user cares about ease of use.

Marketing and sales must work together In B2B, marketing generates leads and sales closes them. When these teams are misaligned — disagreeing on what counts as a "qualified lead" or blaming each other for missed targets — revenue suffers. The best companies have tight feedback loops between marketing and sales.

The Marketing Funnel

B2B marketers think in terms of a funnel — a model for how strangers become customers:

  • Top of Funnel (ToFu) — Awareness: The prospect doesn't know you exist yet. Content marketing, SEO, social media, and paid ads bring them in.
  • Middle of Funnel (MoFu) — Consideration: The prospect knows your product exists and is evaluating it. Webinars, case studies, whitepapers, and email nurture campaigns keep them engaged.
  • Bottom of Funnel (BoFu) — Decision: The prospect is close to buying. Free trials, demos, ROI calculators, and direct sales outreach push them over the line.

Key Marketing Channels in B2B

Content Marketing

Creating blog posts, whitepapers, ebooks, videos, and webinars that attract and educate potential buyers. Content marketing is a long-term play — it builds organic search traffic and positions the company as a trusted expert.

Email Marketing

Sending targeted emails to lists of prospects and customers. Email is one of the highest-ROI channels in B2B. "Nurture sequences" — automated series of emails sent over weeks or months — keep prospects engaged until they're ready to buy.

Paid Advertising

Running ads on Google (search and display), LinkedIn (the dominant B2B social platform), and industry sites. LinkedIn is especially important in B2B because you can target by job title, company size, and industry.

Events & Webinars

Conferences, trade shows, and hosted events are major B2B lead generation channels. They're expensive but effective for relationship-building and generating high-quality leads.

SEO (Search Engine Optimization)

Getting your website to rank on Google for terms your buyers search for. Enterprise buyers often start their research on Google — showing up at the top of relevant searches is a high-value, low-cost lead source over time.

The MarTech Stack

The MarTech stack (marketing technology stack) is the collection of software tools a marketing team uses to do its job. A typical enterprise MarTech stack includes:

Marketing Automation Platform (MAP)

The central tool for running campaigns, managing email sequences, and scoring leads. The MAP tracks which web pages a prospect visited, which emails they opened, and how engaged they are — and uses that data to determine when to hand a lead to sales. Major vendors: HubSpot, Marketo (Adobe), Pardot (Salesforce), Eloqua (Oracle).

CMS (Content Management System)

The software that powers the company website. Marketing teams use a CMS to publish blog posts, landing pages, and product pages without needing a developer for every change. Major vendors: WordPress, Webflow, Contentful, Sitecore.

Analytics Platform

Tools that track website traffic, campaign performance, and conversion rates. Google Analytics is the most common, but enterprise teams often add tools like Mixpanel or Amplitude for more detailed product analytics.

Ad Tech

Tools for running and managing paid advertising campaigns. Google Ads for search, LinkedIn Campaign Manager for B2B targeting, and demand-side platforms (DSPs) for programmatic display advertising.

ABM (Account-Based Marketing) Tools

ABM is a strategy where marketing focuses resources on a specific list of target accounts rather than casting a wide net. ABM tools help identify and engage specific companies. Major vendors: 6sense, Demandbase, Terminus.

Lead Scoring and MQLs

Not every lead is ready to buy. Marketing teams use lead scoring — assigning points based on a prospect's behavior (visited the pricing page: +10, opened 3 emails: +5, from a target industry: +10) — to prioritize who gets passed to sales.

When a lead hits a score threshold, it becomes an MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead) — a lead that marketing considers ready for sales outreach. Sales then determines if it's an SQL (Sales Qualified Lead) worth pursuing.

The MQL/SQL handoff is a common friction point Marketing often thinks they're sending good leads; sales often thinks the leads are unqualified. Defining the exact criteria for an MQL — and agreeing on it between teams — is one of the most important things a B2B company can do.

Key Marketing Metrics

  • MQLs generated — how many qualified leads marketing produced
  • CPL (Cost Per Lead) — how much it costs to generate one lead
  • Conversion rate — % of visitors who take a desired action (fill a form, start a trial)
  • CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) — total marketing + sales spend divided by new customers won
  • Marketing-sourced revenue — % of closed deals that originated from a marketing campaign
  • Email open rate / click rate — engagement metrics for email campaigns
