Module 1

How Enterprise Businesses Operate

~15 min read  ·  8-question quiz

What Is an Enterprise?

The word enterprise is used loosely, but in a business context it typically means a large organization — usually with 1,000+ employees, significant revenue (often $100M+), and operations that span multiple teams, offices, or even countries.

Enterprises are different from small and medium businesses (SMBs) in a few important ways:

  • Scale: Many employees, business units, and geographies to coordinate.
  • Complexity: More processes, more approvals, more systems running simultaneously.
  • Buying behavior: Purchasing decisions involve multiple stakeholders and can take months or years.
  • Software investment: Enterprises spend heavily on software because even small efficiency gains multiply across thousands of employees.
Why this matters for you As a consultant at a company selling enterprise software, you're selling to these large organizations. Understanding how they're structured helps you understand who you're talking to, what they care about, and why their buying process moves slowly.

The B2B vs. B2C Distinction

B2B (business-to-business) means one company sells products or services to another company. B2C (business-to-consumer) means a company sells directly to individual people.

Enterprise software is almost always B2B. A company like Salesforce sells its CRM platform to other companies — not to individual consumers. This distinction shapes everything: pricing, sales cycles, product design, and support.

Core Departments in an Enterprise

Large businesses are organized into departments, each with a specific function. Here are the major ones you'll encounter:

Sales

Responsible for generating revenue by closing deals with customers. Enterprise sales teams are large and highly structured, with roles like Sales Development Representatives (SDRs), Account Executives (AEs), and Sales Managers. They track their work in a CRM.

Marketing

Generates awareness and demand for the company's products. Marketing creates content, runs campaigns, manages the website, and passes leads to the sales team. They use a stack of tools called the MarTech (marketing technology) stack.

Finance

Manages the company's money — accounts payable, accounts receivable, budgeting, forecasting, and financial reporting. Finance uses tools like ERP systems and accounting software to track every dollar in and out.

Operations

Keeps the business running day-to-day. In a manufacturing company, operations manages the supply chain and production. In a software company, it might mean managing internal processes and vendor relationships.

IT (Information Technology)

Manages the company's technology infrastructure — hardware, software, security, and data. IT is often the team that evaluates and approves new software purchases. They care deeply about security, integration with existing systems, and uptime.

Human Resources (HR)

Manages hiring, compensation, benefits, performance reviews, and compliance. HR uses its own category of software called HCM (Human Capital Management) or HRIS (Human Resources Information System).

Customer Success / Support

In software companies, this team manages the relationship with customers after they've purchased. Their goal is to make sure customers get value, renew their contracts, and don't churn (cancel). They use ticketing systems and CS platforms to do this.

How the C-Suite Works

The C-suite refers to the top executives whose titles start with "Chief." Each is responsible for a major department or function:

  • CEO — Chief Executive Officer. Runs the company overall.
  • CFO — Chief Financial Officer. Owns all things finance.
  • CTO — Chief Technology Officer. Oversees the tech/product direction.
  • CRO — Chief Revenue Officer. Owns sales, sometimes marketing.
  • CMO — Chief Marketing Officer. Owns marketing.
  • COO — Chief Operating Officer. Manages day-to-day operations.
  • CISO — Chief Information Security Officer. Owns security strategy.

In enterprise software sales, deals often require sign-off from a C-suite executive. Understanding which "C" cares about your product is key.

How Departments Interact

Departments don't operate in isolation — they pass information and work to each other constantly. Here are the most important handoffs:

  • Marketing → Sales: Marketing generates leads (potential customers), which it hands off to the sales team to follow up and close.
  • Sales → Finance: Once a deal is closed, finance processes the contract, invoices the customer, and tracks revenue.
  • Sales → Customer Success: After signing, the customer is handed to a CS team who onboards them and manages the ongoing relationship.
  • IT → Everyone: IT sets the rules for what software can be used, manages access, and ensures systems are secure and integrated.
Key insight: software mirrors org structure Enterprise software systems are almost always built around one department's workflow. A CRM is built for Sales. A marketing automation tool is built for Marketing. An ERP is built for Finance and Operations. This is why enterprises end up with dozens of different systems — each department has its own.

Business Metrics You'll Hear

Every department tracks metrics (numbers that measure performance). Some common ones across the business:

  • ARR / MRR — Annual/Monthly Recurring Revenue. How much subscription revenue the company earns each year or month.
  • Churn Rate — The percentage of customers who cancel. High churn is a red flag.
  • CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost. How much it costs to win one new customer (marketing + sales spend).
  • LTV — Lifetime Value. How much revenue a customer generates over the entire relationship.
  • Pipeline — The total value of deals the sales team is actively working on.
  • Win Rate — The percentage of deals in the pipeline that the company closes.
  • NPS — Net Promoter Score. A measure of customer satisfaction and loyalty.

The Role of Enterprise Software

Large businesses generate enormous amounts of data and activity — millions of transactions, thousands of customer interactions, hundreds of employees. Without software to organize and automate this, nothing would work at scale.

Enterprise software generally falls into a few major categories:

  • CRM (Customer Relationship Management) — tracks customers and sales activity
  • ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) — manages finance, supply chain, operations
  • HCM/HRIS — manages people and HR processes
  • MarTech — tools for marketing campaigns, content, and analytics
  • BI / Analytics — business intelligence tools that turn data into insights
  • Integration platforms — software that connects all the other systems together

You'll learn about each of these in detail in later modules. For now, the important thing to understand is that enterprises run on a complex web of interconnected systems — and consultants are often hired to help businesses choose, implement, and optimize these systems.
