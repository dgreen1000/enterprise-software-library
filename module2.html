Module 2

Enterprise Sales & the B2B Sales Cycle

~20 min read  ·  16-question quiz

Why B2B Sales Is Different

When you buy something as a consumer — a pair of shoes, a Netflix subscription — the decision is quick, the price is fixed, and you make it alone. B2B sales, especially at the enterprise level, is almost the opposite.

  • Multiple decision-makers: A single enterprise deal might need sign-off from the end user, their manager, IT, finance, legal, and a C-suite executive.
  • Long sales cycles: Enterprise deals can take 3–18 months from first conversation to signed contract.
  • High deal values: Enterprise software contracts are often $50,000–$1,000,000+ per year.
  • Customization and negotiation: Unlike a consumer app, enterprise pricing and contract terms are usually negotiated.
  • Risk aversion: A bad software purchase at an enterprise affects thousands of employees and can cost millions to fix. Buyers move slowly because the stakes are high.
The core challenge of enterprise sales You're not just convincing one person — you're building consensus across an entire organization while managing a complex process that can stall at any stage.

Sales Team Roles

Enterprise sales teams have distinct roles, each responsible for a different part of the process:

SDR — Sales Development Representative

SDRs are typically early-career salespeople whose job is prospecting — finding potential customers and making first contact (cold calls, emails, LinkedIn outreach). Their goal is to book a meeting for an Account Executive. SDRs don't close deals; they open conversations.

AE — Account Executive

AEs own the full sales cycle from the first qualified meeting to the signed contract. They run demos, handle objections, build relationships, write proposals, and negotiate terms. AEs are measured on quota — a revenue target they're expected to hit each quarter.

SE — Sales Engineer (also called Solutions Engineer)

SEs are technical experts who partner with AEs on complex deals. When a prospect asks deep technical questions about how the software works, integrates with their systems, or handles their data, the SE answers them. SEs build trust with the buyer's IT and technical teams.

Sales Manager / VP of Sales

Managers coach AEs, review pipeline, approve discounts, and are accountable for their team hitting quota. The VP of Sales owns the entire revenue number for the organization and sets strategy.

RevOps — Revenue Operations

A newer role that manages the tools, data, and processes that support the sales team. RevOps ensures the CRM is clean and accurate, that reporting works, and that salespeople have what they need to do their jobs efficiently.

The B2B Sales Cycle — Stage by Stage

Every company names these stages slightly differently in their CRM, but the underlying process is consistent across enterprise software sales.

Stage 1: Prospecting

The SDR identifies companies that could be a good fit (based on size, industry, existing tech, etc.) and reaches out. This is called outbound sales. If a potential customer contacts the company first (fills out a form, attends a webinar), that's inbound.

Stage 2: Discovery

The first real conversation. The AE's goal is to understand the prospect's situation: What problems do they have? What are they currently using? Who is involved in the decision? What does success look like to them? Good discovery is what separates great salespeople from mediocre ones — the best salespeople listen more than they talk.

Stage 3: Demo / Evaluation

The AE (and often the SE) shows the prospect how the product works, tailored to their specific use case. The prospect may then enter a proof of concept (POC) or pilot — a time-limited trial of the product in their environment.

Stage 4: Proposal

The AE puts together a formal proposal: pricing, contract length, implementation details, and terms. At the enterprise level, prospects often issue an RFP (Request for Proposal) — a formal document asking multiple vendors to submit detailed proposals so they can compare.

Stage 5: Negotiation

Price, contract length, legal terms, security requirements — everything gets negotiated. Legal teams on both sides review and redline (mark up) contracts. This stage can add weeks or months to a deal.

Stage 6: Close

The contract is signed. This is called a closed-won deal. If the prospect decides not to buy (or to buy from a competitor), it's closed-lost. The AE logs both outcomes in the CRM with a reason.

Understanding Stakeholders

One of the hardest parts of enterprise sales is navigating the many people involved in a purchase decision. Each plays a different role:

Champion

The person inside the prospect's company who wants your product to win. They'll advocate for you internally, share information you wouldn't otherwise have, and help you navigate the organization. Finding and developing a champion is often the most important thing an AE can do.

Economic Buyer

The person who controls the budget and ultimately signs off on the spend. This is often a VP or C-suite executive. You may not talk to them until late in the deal, but you need to reach them — deals that never get to the economic buyer often stall and die.

End Users

The people who will actually use the software day-to-day. Their enthusiasm (or resistance) matters — a great product that users hate will churn.

IT / Security

IT must approve any software that touches their infrastructure, data, or network. Security teams review how data is stored and transmitted. These reviews can take weeks and are a common source of deal delays.

Legal / Procurement

Legal reviews contract terms. Procurement (a department at large enterprises) manages the vendor relationship and often negotiates pricing.

MEDDIC: a common framework for qualifying deals Many enterprise sales teams use MEDDIC to evaluate whether a deal is real: Metrics (what value does the customer expect?), Economic Buyer (have you reached them?), Decision Criteria (how will they choose?), Decision Process (what are the steps to get a decision?), Identify Pain (what problem are they solving?), Champion (who is advocating internally?). If any letter is weak, the deal is at risk.

Key Sales Metrics

Sales leaders live and die by metrics. Here are the most important ones:

  • Quota: The revenue target each AE is expected to hit in a given period (usually quarterly or annually).
  • Pipeline: The total value of all deals an AE or team is actively working on. "3x pipeline coverage" means a rep has $3M in pipeline to hit a $1M quota.
  • Win Rate: The percentage of deals in the pipeline that are won. A 25% win rate means closing 1 in 4 deals.
  • ACV (Annual Contract Value): The average yearly value of a deal. Helps benchmark deal size.
  • Sales Cycle Length: The average number of days from first meeting to close. Shortening this improves efficiency.
  • Ramp Time: How long it takes a new AE to reach full productivity. Usually 6–12 months for enterprise roles.
  • Forecast: The AE's (or manager's) prediction of which deals will close in the current quarter. Forecasting accuracy is a sign of sales discipline.

Sales Methodologies

Enterprise sales organizations often train their teams on a formal methodology — a structured approach to running deals. A few common ones:

  • MEDDIC / MEDDPICC: A qualification framework (described above). Ensures reps are working deals that can actually close.
  • Challenger Sale: Teaches reps to challenge the prospect's thinking, reframe their problem, and show them a new way of seeing the world. Works well for complex software where the buyer doesn't fully understand the solution.
  • SPIN Selling: Focuses on asking the right questions — Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff — to help the prospect articulate their own pain before presenting a solution.
  • Sandler: Emphasizes mutual qualification — both the buyer and seller should decide if it's a fit. Known for the "pain funnel" and avoiding wasted time on deals that won't close.

Market Segments: Enterprise vs. Mid-Market vs. SMB

Not all B2B sales is "enterprise." Most software companies divide their market into segments with different sales motions:

  • SMB (Small/Medium Business): Deals under ~$10K/year. Usually high-volume, fast cycles, often self-serve (the buyer signs up online without ever talking to a salesperson).
  • Mid-Market: Deals in the $10K–$100K/year range. Shorter sales cycles than enterprise, fewer stakeholders, but still requires a sales rep.
  • Enterprise: Deals over $100K/year, sometimes millions. Long cycles, many stakeholders, heavy negotiation, professional services often included.

Your company may have separate teams for each segment, with different products, pricing models, and sales processes tailored to each.
