Module 5

E-commerce & B2B Digital Commerce

~15 min read  ·  8-question quiz

E-commerce in a B2B Context

When most people think of e-commerce, they think of Amazon or Shopify stores — consumers buying products online. But B2B e-commerce — businesses buying from other businesses online — is actually a much larger market and is growing rapidly.

Traditionally, B2B purchasing was handled entirely through salespeople: a buyer would call a rep, negotiate pricing, and place orders via phone, fax, or email. That model is being replaced by digital self-service — buyers want to research, configure, price, and purchase online, just like they do in their personal lives.

B2B buyers are changing A growing percentage of B2B buyers are millennials who grew up shopping online. They prefer to research independently, get pricing without talking to a salesperson, and complete purchases digitally. Companies that can't support this expect to lose deals to competitors who can.

B2B vs. B2C E-commerce

B2B digital commerce is more complex than consumer e-commerce in several ways:

  • Negotiated pricing: B2B buyers often have custom pricing based on volume, contract terms, or their relationship with the vendor. The price a buyer sees online may be specific to their account.
  • Complex products: B2B products often need configuration — selecting options, sizes, quantities, and specifications — before pricing can be calculated.
  • Purchase approval workflows: Enterprise buyers often need manager approval before completing a purchase. The e-commerce system may need to support multi-step approval chains.
  • Multiple buyers per account: A single company might have dozens of people who can place orders, each with different permissions and spending limits.
  • Invoicing and payment terms: B2B buyers often pay on 30-, 60-, or 90-day terms via invoice rather than credit card at checkout.

Selling Models in B2B Commerce

Direct (Manufacturer to Buyer)

A manufacturer or vendor sells directly to enterprise customers through their own website or sales team. This gives maximum control over pricing and customer experience but requires investment in sales and commerce infrastructure.

Through Distributors / Resellers

Many B2B products are sold through a network of distributors or resellers who buy in bulk and resell to end customers. Enterprise software often uses channel partners or VARs (Value-Added Resellers) who add implementation services on top of the software.

Marketplace

Platforms like Amazon Business, Alibaba, or industry-specific marketplaces aggregate many vendors in one place. Buyers can compare options and purchase from multiple vendors in a single transaction.

Major E-commerce Platforms

Shopify / Shopify Plus

Originally a B2C platform, Shopify has expanded its B2B capabilities. Shopify Plus (the enterprise tier) supports wholesale pricing, customer-specific catalogs, and B2B checkout flows. It's popular with mid-market companies for its ease of use.

Magento (Adobe Commerce)

An open-source platform with deep B2B capabilities — custom pricing, quote management, purchase approval workflows, and company account management. More complex to implement than Shopify but highly customizable.

commercetools

A modern, API-first commerce platform built for enterprises that need maximum flexibility. Rather than a traditional monolithic storefront, commercetools provides building blocks (APIs) that developers assemble into a custom commerce experience.

SAP Commerce Cloud / Salesforce Commerce Cloud

Enterprise-grade platforms integrated with their parent ecosystems (SAP ERP, Salesforce CRM). Chosen by large enterprises that want tight integration between their commerce and back-office systems.

Headless Commerce

Headless commerce is an architecture where the front-end (what the customer sees) is decoupled from the back-end commerce engine. Instead of buying a pre-built storefront, the company builds its own front-end experience that communicates with the commerce platform via APIs.

This gives enterprises total control over the buyer experience — they can build exactly the interface they want — but it requires more development investment. Platforms like commercetools, Elastic Path, and VTEX are built for this model.

CPQ: Configure, Price, Quote

For complex B2B products, a separate tool called CPQ (Configure, Price, Quote) handles the process of customizing a product, calculating the correct price, and generating a formal quote. CPQ tools integrate with the CRM (so salespeople can build quotes in Salesforce) and the ERP (so pricing rules and inventory are accurate).

Major CPQ vendors include Salesforce CPQ, Conga, and PROS.

Key B2B Commerce Metrics

  • GMV (Gross Merchandise Value) — total value of orders processed through the platform
  • AOV (Average Order Value) — average dollar value per order
  • Conversion rate — % of visitors who complete a purchase
  • Digital self-serve rate — % of orders placed without sales team involvement
  • Reorder rate — how often existing customers come back to buy again
