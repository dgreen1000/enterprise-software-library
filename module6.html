Module 6

ERP Systems

~15 min read  ·  8-question quiz

What Is an ERP?

An ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) system is the operational backbone of a large company. It's a centralized software platform that manages and integrates the core business processes that keep a company running: finance, supply chain, inventory, manufacturing, procurement, and often HR.

The defining feature of an ERP is that it's a single system of record for operational data. Rather than having a separate system for finance, a separate one for inventory, and another for purchasing — all potentially out of sync — an ERP puts everything in one place. A purchase order, for example, automatically updates inventory levels, triggers an accounts payable entry, and creates a record in the general ledger — all in one transaction.

ERP vs. CRM The easiest way to remember the difference: a CRM manages customer-facing activity (sales, relationships, revenue). An ERP manages internal operations (finance, inventory, supply chain). They often integrate with each other — when a deal closes in the CRM, the contract flows into the ERP for invoicing and revenue recognition.

Core ERP Modules

Most ERP systems are modular — companies can implement the pieces they need. Common modules include:

Financial Management

The general ledger, accounts payable (money the company owes), accounts receivable (money owed to the company), budgeting, and financial reporting. This is the most universally used ERP module — virtually every ERP implementation includes finance.

Supply Chain Management

Managing the flow of goods from suppliers to customers. This includes procurement (buying from vendors), warehouse management, logistics, and demand planning. Critical for manufacturers, distributors, and retailers.

Inventory Management

Tracking stock levels across warehouses, automating reorder points, and managing product locations. An inventory module ensures the company always knows what it has on hand and where it is.

Manufacturing / Production

Managing production schedules, bills of materials (the components that go into a product), work orders, and quality control. Essential for manufacturing companies.

Human Capital Management (HCM)

Managing employee records, payroll, benefits, and time tracking. Some ERPs include a full HCM module; others integrate with a dedicated HR system.

Project Management

For service businesses (like consulting firms), the ERP may include project management capabilities — tracking time, costs, and profitability by project.

Major ERP Vendors

SAP

The largest ERP vendor in the world. SAP S/4HANA is their flagship cloud ERP, used by many of the world's largest companies. SAP is powerful but notoriously complex and expensive to implement — SAP implementations often cost millions of dollars and take years. There's a massive ecosystem of SAP consultants and implementation partners.

Oracle (Oracle Cloud ERP / Oracle Fusion)

SAP's primary competitor at the enterprise level. Oracle Cloud ERP is a full-featured cloud platform strong in finance, supply chain, and manufacturing. Also well-known for its database technology that many ERPs run on.

NetSuite (Oracle NetSuite)

The leading cloud ERP for mid-market companies. NetSuite is more affordable and faster to implement than SAP or Oracle, making it the go-to choice for growing companies that have outgrown QuickBooks. Acquired by Oracle in 2016.

Microsoft Dynamics 365

Microsoft's ERP (and CRM) platform. Tightly integrated with the Microsoft ecosystem — Azure, Teams, Office 365. Popular with companies already committed to Microsoft infrastructure.

Infor, Epicor, IFS

Industry-specific ERPs targeting manufacturing, healthcare, construction, and field services. They compete by offering deep functionality for specific verticals that generalist ERPs may not cover as well.

Why ERP Implementations Are Hard

ERP implementations are among the most complex and risky IT projects a company can undertake. Common reasons they fail or go over budget:

  • Data migration: Moving years of data from legacy systems into the new ERP, cleaning it, and ensuring it's accurate is enormously time-consuming.
  • Change management: An ERP changes how nearly every department does its job. Getting thousands of employees to adopt new workflows is a massive organizational challenge.
  • Customization creep: Companies try to replicate their existing processes exactly in the new system, which requires expensive custom development that makes future upgrades difficult.
  • Integration complexity: The ERP needs to talk to dozens of other systems — the CRM, e-commerce platform, HR system, and more.
The "vanilla implementation" principle Experienced ERP consultants often advise clients to adopt the ERP's standard processes rather than customizing the system to match old workflows. It's cheaper, faster, and easier to upgrade — but it requires the client to change how they work, which is politically difficult.
